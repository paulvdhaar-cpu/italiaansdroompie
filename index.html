Our Story

Where a family
fell in love with Italy

David and Lisette weren't looking for a project. They were looking for a life — one where their three children grew up with dirty knees and neighbours who became friends. When they first drove up the hill to San Procolo and saw the valley below, they didn't need to discuss it.

They've kept it small on purpose. Thirty pitches, a handful of tents, a pool with a view. Nothing that needs managing from a distance. Most evenings you'll find David somewhere on site — he'll probably invite you to sit down for a glass of Centanni wine before you've had a chance to say no.

A proper campsite, not a resort

Kids roam freely here. Parents actually unwind. The mornings begin with fresh bread on the terrace and the days end with fireflies and that particular stillness that only seems to exist in the Italian hills.

Morning

Wake to the sound
of cicadas

Terracotta-warm morning light comes in slow. Espresso under the olive trees. The bread arrives still warm and the day hasn't properly started yet — and already you don't want it to end.

Afternoon

Harvest olives.
Eat dinner slow.

No agenda. The pool glitters against the valley. Children disappear into the olive grove. You find yourself doing nothing in particular and feeling, somehow, completely full.

Evening

The Adriatic
can wait.

It's twenty minutes away and you'll go tomorrow. Tonight, David opens the wine and the long table fills with people who arrived as strangers. There is no rush here. That is the whole point.

Life on the Hill

Fill your days
the Italian way

No agenda. No entertainment schedule. Just a hilltop, a swimming pool and 20 minutes to the Adriatic. The children will figure out the rest — and you'll be amazed what a week without screens does for all of you.

On the hillside
Morning ritual

Cornetti still warm, olive oil and bread, espresso under the trees. It becomes a ritual by day two, an addiction by day four.

On the hillside
Afternoons by the pool

The valley spreads below, hazy in the heat. Children in the water. You with a book, or nothing at all.

On the hillside
Nature & butterflies

Something about the hillside draws children outside and keeps them there. The butterflies help. So do the olive groves, the lavender paths, the sense that something might be hiding around every corner.

On the hillside
Italian evenings

Twice a week David and Lisette set up a long table. Neighbours who arrived as strangers. Local food, Centanni wine, and somehow still talking at midnight.

20 minutes away
Adriatic beach days

Grottammare in 20 minutes. The kind of sandy beach where Italians still outnumber tourists. Gelato on the way back is not optional.

25 km away
Sibillini mountains

Peaks at 2476m, wildflower valleys, trails that make you forget the drive.

Nearby
Medieval hilltowns

Fermo, Ascoli Piceno, Loreto. Marble squares, no tour groups, lunches that run into afternoon. David will tell you where to go.

On the hillside
Playground & table tennis

Playhouse, slide, swings and a large trampoline. Table tennis that always draws a crowd by late afternoon.

The Region

Le Marche —
where you can still find yourself

Le Marche sits between the Apennines and the Adriatic and somehow most tourists still miss it. That's not going to last — but for now, you'll have the hilltowns mostly to yourself, and the beaches still feel like Italy in the way Italy used to feel.
